Analyzing US-Government Gathered Data from the Vietnam War¶

Ryan Brown¶

Introduction¶

The goal of this project is to make use of data gathered by the United States Government and its allies throughout the first few years of the Vietnam War to explore what activities the enemy performed, and which time and to what success. The data in question is from the Viet Cong Initiated Incidents Files, otherwise known as "VCII," from 1 January 1965 to 31 December 1968. This data, collected and compiled by the Department of Defense (DoD) during the first few years of America's official war in the Republic of Vietnam, was used to determine how well pacification efforts were going in the various provinces of the Republic of Vietnam (RVN) and to track the activities of the enemy "Viet Cong," short for "Vietnamese Communists" which is meant to represent both North Vietnamese Army regulars and local Main Force insurgents as well as smaller-level local insurgent militias. This data, split into two large tables, contains an entry for every reported enemy (Viet Cong) initiated attack against the Republic of Vietnam, US forces, and their allies. With every entry comes a unique identifier, time and date of the incident, location (coordinates), number of enemy wounded, captured, or killed, number of friendlies wounded, captured, or killed, the country in which the incident occurred, and the type of activity committed by the Viet Cong (including combat, propaganda, terrorism, etc). My goal with this data is to import the data, which was helpfully changed from the old NIPS system to a tab-delimited format, sanitize it, remove unnecessary variables, change different indices and codes for locations to more readable values (ex: change Province values to the actual Province name in string format), merge both datasets together, and determine which province was the most violent (had the highest number of combat-related incidents) during 1968, one of the most crucial years of America's involvement in the Vietnam War.

Background Information¶

Since this project can get heavy on historical and military lingo, I have included some phrases and their definition for additional context.

Corps¶

The Army of the Republic of Vietnam (ARVN) was split into four Corps, or a large collection of division-sized elements, which ran down the length of the country. These corps were used also as "Corps Tactical Zones" to represent which general area of the country one was talking about, with "I Corps" representing the northern-most provinces of South Vietnam which bordered warring North Vietnam, "II Corps" representing the large central highlands region of the country, "III Corps" representing the capital Saigon and key regions around the capital up to the Cambodian border, and "IV Corps," the region that is characterized by the delta of the massive Mekong River. Attached is a map taken from the [Wikipedia Article for I Corps] (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I_Corps_(South_Vietnam)) which helps to provide some visual context, including the province names, for viewers.

Next up, I will be transforming all the entries Date and Time columns into Pandas "Datetime" format, which is a standardized time format within Pandas. To make the conversion from a string to datetime go smoother, I append the string '19' to the front of all the dates within the Date column to indicate that this data is taking place within the 20th century, and because the data only recorded the year up to the double digits, and datetime may get confused thinking that we are talking about data in the 21st century. The time string was also originally in the format ("####"), but I changed that to "(##:##") for readability. I then used the pd.to_datetime function to convert our Date column data into Pandas datetime, telling it that the format is Full year number, month, and day.

Conclusions¶

Looking at the combat incidents by day graph, we can see that there is an insane spike of events that occurs in May. This may correspond to the [Mini Tet Offensive] (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/May_offensive), but a spike in incidents of this scale seems to be abnormal. Other than that spike in May, we can see that the first few months of 1968 were full of combat-related incidents, which correspond to the famous [Tet Offensive] (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tet_Offensive) of 1968. This offensive saw most population centers and military installations throughout South Vietnam targetted by North Vietnamese soldiers or local South Vietnamese Communist forces. The province graph seems to show that the five most "active" provinces of South Vietnam are Quang Nam, Hua Nghia, Tay Ninh, Quang Tri, and Thua Thien. Quang Nam, Quang Tri, and Thua Thien are the three northern-most provinces of South Vietnam and were the sites of many violent battles during the course of the Tet Offensive. Tay Ninh and Hua Nghia are two provinces between the capital of Saigon and the Cambodian border. Finally, a simple graph of counting incidents by corps shows that III Corps had the most incidents tallied overall, IV Corps follows behind, then I and II Corps. I personally know very little about the course of the war in IV Corps, but it always struck me as a less active, or possibly less-covered, theater of the war. I will be sure to put more research into how the war developed in the Mekong Delta region as a result of this data analysis!
